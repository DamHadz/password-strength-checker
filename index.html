      What is Password Strength?

      Password strength is a measure of how difficult it would be for an attacker to guess or crack a password through brute force, dictionary attacks, or other methods. A strong password combines multiple character types (uppercase, lowercase, numbers, symbols), has sufficient length, and avoids predictable patterns.

      Password strength is typically measured by two key factors: entropy (the randomness or unpredictability of the password) and resistance to common attack vectors. A password with 80+ bits of entropy is considered very strong by current cryptographic standards.

      How Hackers Crack Passwords

      Understanding attack methods helps you build better defenses. Here are the most common techniques:

      💥

      Brute Force

      Systematically tries every possible combination. A modern GPU cluster can test 100 billion passwords per second. Short passwords fall instantly.

      📖

      Dictionary Attack

      Uses wordlists of common passwords, phrases, and their variations. The RockYou list alone has 14 million passwords. "Password123" is in every list.

      🌈

      Rainbow Tables

      Pre-computed hash tables allowing instant lookup of password hashes. Unsalted MD5 hashes can be cracked in milliseconds using these tables.

      🎭

      Credential Stuffing

      Uses username/password combos from previous breaches. With billions of records leaked, password reuse is extremely dangerous.

      🎣

      Phishing

      Tricks users into entering credentials on fake sites. No amount of password strength protects against willingly entering your password on a malicious page.

      🔑

      Keylogging

      Malware that captures keystrokes as you type. A perfect password is useless if attackers can watch you type it in real time.

      10 Tips to Create Secure Passwords

      01

      Use a Passphrase

      Combine 4+ random words: correct-horse-battery-staple. Long passphrases are easy to remember but extremely hard to crack — they beat short complex passwords.

      02

      Aim for 16+ Characters

      Every additional character exponentially increases crack time. A 16-character password takes millions of years to brute force at current computing speeds.

      03

      Mix Character Types

      Include uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols. This expands your character set from 26 to 95, making brute force attack space 86 trillion times larger.

      04

      Never Reuse Passwords

      Each account needs a unique password. One breach shouldn't compromise all your accounts. Use a password manager to handle the complexity.

      05

      Use a Password Manager

      Tools like Bitwarden, 1Password, or Dashlane generate and store unique passwords for every site. You only need to remember one master password.

      06

      Enable Two-Factor Authentication

      2FA adds a second verification step. Even if your password is stolen, attackers still need your phone or authenticator app to gain access.

      07

      Avoid Personal Info

      Names, birthdays, pet names, and addresses are easy to guess through social engineering. Attackers often research targets on social media before attacking.

      08

      Skip Predictable Substitutions

      Changing "a" to "@" or "o" to "0" (leet speak) is well-known to hackers. Modern dictionary attacks include all common substitution variants.

      09

      Check for Breaches

      Use services like Have I Been Pwned to see if your email or passwords appeared in known data breaches. Change compromised passwords immediately.

      10

      Update Periodically

      Change critical account passwords every 6–12 months. Banking, email, and work accounts should be updated more frequently for maximum security.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      Is it safe to type my password into this checker?

      Yes. All password analysis happens entirely in your browser using JavaScript. Your password is never sent to any server, logged, or stored. You can verify this by checking the browser's network tab — no requests are made when you type.

      What makes a password truly uncrackable?

      No password is truly uncrackable forever — computing power grows over time. However, a random 20+ character password with mixed types would take longer than the age of the universe to crack with current technology.

      How accurate is the crack time estimate?

      Estimates are based on offline brute-force attacks using ~10 billion guesses per second (a GPU cluster). Real-world times vary depending on attack method, hardware, and whether common patterns are used.
